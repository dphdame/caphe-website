## SLIDE 5: The Question CEA Answers
**[4:15-6:15 | 2 min]**

At its core, cost-effectiveness analysis answers one question: given our limited resources, which option gives us the most health improvement per dollar spent?

Here's a simple scenario. Say we have a million dollars and two options: expand diabetes screening, or fund a community walking program. Both are good programs. Both will improve health. But they cost different amounts and produce different outcomes. How do we compare them?

That's what CEA does. It gives us a common metric so we can make apples-to-apples comparisons across very different types of programs.

## SLIDE 6: What CEA Is and Isn't
**[6:15-8:15 | 2 min]**

Before we go further, let's be clear about what CEA can and can't do.

CEA is a tool for comparing value across interventions. It summarizes costs and outcomes into a single ratio we can use for comparison.

What it's not: a decision-maker. CEA informs decisions; it doesn't make them. It's also not a judgment of human worth. When we say a program isn't cost-effective, we're making a statement about efficiency, not about whether the people it serves matter.

And critically, CEA is not the only consideration. Equity, feasibility, political context; all of these matter. We'll come back to that later.

## SLIDE 8: The ICER Formula
**[8:30-11:30 | 3 min]**

This is the formula at the heart of cost-effectiveness analysis. The ICER, or Incremental Cost-Effectiveness Ratio.

ICER equals the cost of the new program minus the cost of current practice, divided by the health effect of the new program minus the health effect of current practice.

Let's break that down. The numerator is the incremental cost: how much MORE does the new option cost compared to what we're already doing? The denominator is the incremental effect: how much MORE health do we gain?

The result tells us: for each additional unit of health we want to gain, how much extra do we have to pay?

Two things to notice. First, we're comparing to current practice, not to doing nothing. That's important. Second, the "unit of health" can be different things depending on the analysis: cases prevented, life-years gained, quality-adjusted life-years. We'll talk about those choices shortly.

## SLIDE 9: Worked Example 1 - Diabetes Screening
**[11:30-14:00 | 2.5 min]**

Let's try a simple example. Imagine our county is considering expanding diabetes screening to a new population.

Current practice costs $500,000 a year and prevents 50 cases. The expanded screening would cost $800,000 a year and prevent 80 cases.

So the incremental cost is $800,000 minus $500,000, which is $300,000. The incremental effect is 80 minus 50, which is 30 additional cases prevented.

The ICER: $300,000 divided by 30 equals $10,000 per case prevented.

Now, is $10,000 per case prevented a good deal? That's where it gets interesting. We'd need to know: what does an untreated diabetes case cost the health system over time? What else could we do with that $300,000? Are there equity considerations?

The ICER gives us a starting point for that conversation.

## SLIDE 11: The Measurement Problem
**[14:15-16:15 | 2 min]**

Here's the challenge. Program A prevents 100 deaths. Program B prevents 500 hospitalizations. Program C improves quality of life for 1,000 people.

Which program is "best"? We can't compare these directly because they're measuring different things. Deaths, hospitalizations, and quality of life are all important. They're measured in different units.

To compare across conditions and interventions, we need a common measure that captures both how long people live and how well they live. That's where standardized outcome measures come in.

## SLIDE 12: Outcome Measures Comparison
**[16:15-18:45 | 2.5 min]**

Here's a quick overview of the main options.

Lives saved: the simplest measure. It only captures mortality. Good for interventions where life-or-death is the main outcome, like emergency response.

Life-years gained: a step up. It accounts for age; saving a 30-year-old adds more life-years than saving an 80-year-old. Useful for comparing across age groups, but it doesn't capture quality of life.

Cases prevented: disease-specific. Great for single-disease programs like screening, but hard to compare a case of diabetes prevented to a case of depression prevented.

Hospitalizations averted: very practical for county-level work, especially when we're thinking about system capacity and budget impact.

QALYs: quality-adjusted life-years. This is the one we see most in published CEA research. It combines both how long people live and how well. We'll dig into this one next.

DALYs: disability-adjusted life-years. Similar concept but used more in global health.

No measure is perfect. We pick the one that fits our question, our audience, and our data.

---

## SLIDE 13: QALYs Explained
**[18:45-21:15 | 2.5 min]**

So what's a QALY? One QALY equals one year of life in perfect health. If someone's quality of life is reduced, say by a chronic condition, that year counts as less than one QALY.

Here's the example. A treatment extends someone's life by 5 years, but at a quality level of 0.6, where 1.0 is perfect health and 0 is death. Five years times 0.6 equals 3 QALYs gained.

Why does this matter? Because QALYs let us compare across very different conditions. We can compare a cancer treatment that extends life by 2 years at high quality to a chronic pain intervention that improves quality for 10 years. Without QALYs, those are apples and oranges. With QALYs, we have a common unit.

This is also why most published cost-effectiveness analyses report their ICERs in dollars per QALY. It's become the standard currency for these comparisons.

---

## SLIDE 14: QALY Controversy
**[21:15-23:45 | 2.5 min]**

Now, QALYs are widely used, but we should be honest: they're controversial, and for good reason.

The core concern is this: if a year of life with a disability is rated at, say, 0.5, then the math implies that saving that person's life is "worth less" than saving someone without a disability. That troubles a lot of people, and it should.

Some argue this reflects societal bias against disability and aging. Others argue that patients themselves report lower quality scores, so the measure is capturing real experience. It's an active debate in health economics with no clean resolution.

For our purposes today, here's what matters: know that QALYs exist, understand what they're measuring, and be thoughtful about when and how we use them. For a lot of county-level work, we may not need QALYs at all.

## SLIDE 15: Which Measure Should We Use?
**[23:45-25:45 | 2 min]**

So which measure should we actually use? It depends on the context.

For county-level work, natural units are often most practical. Cases prevented, hospitalizations averted, ED visits avoided. These are concrete, and they're easier to explain to supervisors and policymakers.

For comparing across conditions, QALYs allow apples-to-apples comparisons. If we're deciding between a diabetes program and a mental health program, QALYs can help.

For published research, QALYs are standard. When we're reading the literature, we'll encounter them constantly.

For grant applications, match what the funder expects.

And when in doubt, report multiple measures. Transparency is almost always the right call.

## SLIDE 16: California Example - Diabetes Case Management
**[25:45-29:15 | 3.5 min]**

Now let's look at a real example. This is a diabetes case management program that used nurse case managers and promotora peer educators at community health centers, serving low-income, predominantly Latino patients.

The program cost $1,537 per patient in year one, in 2003 dollars. Among the Medi-Cal cohort of about 1,200 patients, the program reduced HbA1c by half a percentage point and systolic blood pressure by about 2 points.

Those improvements were modeled over a 40-year time horizon to estimate QALYs gained. The result: an ICER of $44,941 per QALY gained for the Medi-Cal population.

Now here's what makes this interesting for a teaching example. That same program, applied to the uninsured cohort, had an ICER of just $10,141 per QALY. Why? Because the uninsured patients started sicker: their HbA1c dropped by 1.3 points instead of 0.5. Same program, bigger clinical improvement, lower ICER.

And for the commercially insured cohort, the ICER was $69,587 per QALY, because those patients started closer to target values. Less room to improve.

Same intervention, same delivery model, three very different ICERs depending on the population. Context matters enormously.

## SLIDE 17: Decision Thresholds
**[29:15-32:45 | 3.5 min]**

So we have an ICER. Now what? How do we decide if $44,941 per QALY is "worth it"?

This is where decision thresholds come in. These are benchmarks researchers and policymakers use as rough guides.

The green zone: below $50,000 per QALY. Generally accepted as good value. Our diabetes case management example for Medi-Cal patients falls here, just barely.

The yellow zone: $50,000 to $150,000 per QALY. Context-dependent. May be acceptable depending on the budget, equity considerations, and what alternatives exist.

The red zone: above $150,000 per QALY. A higher bar to justify. May still be appropriate for equity or other reasons, but we need a strong case.

An important caveat: the United States has no official threshold. These numbers come from decades of research practice and policy discussion. The $50,000 figure, in particular, traces back to an old estimate of the cost of kidney dialysis. Some economists argue thresholds should be higher, $200,000 or more, based on the value of a statistical life.

Thresholds are guidelines. They give us a vocabulary for "is this a good deal?" They complement judgment; they don't replace it.

## SLIDE 18: Beyond Cost-Effectiveness
**[32:45-35:45 | 3 min]**

This is one of the most important slides in the deck: cost-effectiveness is one input to decisions, not the only input.

There are at least five situations where we might fund a program even if it's not the most cost-effective option.

Equity considerations. A program serving underserved populations may be worth a higher cost per outcome. If the alternative is that those communities get nothing, a $100,000-per-QALY program may be the right choice.

Legal or ethical obligations. Some services must be provided regardless of cost-effectiveness. Public health has legal mandates.

Political or community priorities. Values and preferences matter beyond what the math says.

No alternatives exist. An expensive option may still beat doing nothing.

Strategic investments. Building capacity now, say training a promotora workforce, may pay off in ways that a single CEA doesn't capture.

## SLIDE 20: What CEA Doesn't Tell Us
**[36:00-38:30 | 2.5 min]**

CEA has real blind spots, and it's important to name them.

Affordability. Something can be cost-effective but unaffordable. A program at $30,000 per QALY is a great deal, but if it costs $50 million to implement and the budget is $10 million, it doesn't matter. Budget impact analysis is a separate tool that addresses this.

Equity. CEA maximizes total health, not how that health is distributed. It may favor programs that help large populations with small gains over programs that help small, underserved populations with large gains.

Feasibility. A program may look great on paper but be impossible to staff, politically toxic, or dependent on infrastructure that doesn't exist.

Uncertainty. Every ICER is built on assumptions. The models are sensitive to input values, and small changes can shift results.

Political context. Evidence is necessary but rarely sufficient in policy. A perfectly cost-effective program can still lose to a less efficient one with better political support.

## SLIDE 21: Common Pitfalls
**[38:30-41:00 | 2.5 min]**

When we're reading or commissioning CEA work, here are six things to watch for.

First, wrong comparator. A common mistake is comparing a new program to "doing nothing." In practice, we're almost always comparing to current practice. The relevant question isn't "is this better than nothing?" but "is this better than what we're already doing?"

Second, narrow cost perspective. If we only count program costs but ignore costs borne by patients and families, like transportation, lost wages, or childcare, we're understating the true cost.

Third, short time horizon. A diabetes prevention program may look expensive in year one but save millions over a decade. Missing long-term effects biases against prevention.

Fourth, ignoring uncertainty. If a study reports an ICER of $47,000 per QALY without a confidence interval or sensitivity analysis, we should be skeptical. Every estimate has a range.

Fifth, cherry-picking outcomes. If a program is evaluated on five outcomes and only the most favorable one is highlighted, that's a red flag.

Sixth, forgetting context. What's cost-effective in one setting may not be elsewhere. Population demographics, existing infrastructure, and local costs all matter.

## SLIDE 22: Getting Started
**[41:00-43:30 | 2.5 min]**

For those who want to start applying this, here's a practical path forward.

First, don't start from scratch. The Tufts CEA Registry at cear.tuftsmedicalcenter.org is a searchable database of published cost-effectiveness analyses. Before commissioning new work, check whether someone has already studied a similar intervention.

Second, identify the comparator. What's the current practice in our jurisdiction? That's what we're comparing against.

Third, document costs carefully. Include all relevant categories: program delivery, administration, patient costs if we're taking a societal perspective.

Fourth, choose outcomes our audience understands. For a Board of Supervisors presentation, "hospitalizations averted" may land better than "QALYs gained."

Fifth, acknowledge uncertainty. Report ranges alongside point estimates. If our ICER could plausibly be anywhere from $20,000 to $80,000 per QALY, say so.

And sixth, partner with economists or academics if needed. Many universities have health economics faculty who collaborate with public health departments.
