## How to Set Up Polls in Microsoft Teams

1. **During the meeting:** Click the "..." (More) menu in the meeting controls
2. Select **Forms** or **Polls** from the apps
3. Create polls in advance or live during the webinar
4. Polls appear in the chat and attendees can vote directly

## Poll 1: Opening Check-In
**Timing:** After welcome, before content begins (Slide 2-3)

### Poll Question:
**Have you encountered cost-effectiveness analysis in your work?**

- Yes, I've used CEA to inform decisions
- Yes, I've seen CEA studies but haven't conducted one
- I've heard of it but don't know the details
- This is completely new to me

### Discussion Prompt:
"Thanks for sharing where you're starting from. For those who've seen CEA studies, what questions did they help answer? For those new to this, what drew you to this webinar today?"

**Facilitator Note:** This calibrates your depth of explanation. If most are new, slow down on basics. If experienced, focus on nuance.

## Poll 2: The Core Trade-off
**Timing:** After introducing scarcity and opportunity cost (Slide 5)

### Poll Question:
**In your county or organization, what's the most common reason a program gets cut or not funded?**

- Not enough budget overall
- Another program was prioritized instead
- Couldn't demonstrate impact
- Political or leadership changes
- Other (share in chat)

### Discussion Prompt:
"These are all real constraints. CEA helps us make the case for 'prioritized instead' decisions by showing relative value. If you've ever had to choose between two good programs, you've done informal CEA in your head. We're making that process explicit."

## Poll 3: Interpreting the ICER
**Timing:** After the ICER formula slide (Slide 8)

### Poll Question:
**A diabetes screening expansion costs $10,000 per case prevented. Without knowing anything else, your first instinct is:**

- That sounds like a good investment
- That sounds expensive
- I'd need more context to judge
- I have no idea how to evaluate that number

### Discussion Prompt:
"Great range of responses. 'I'd need more context' is actually the right answer. $10,000 per case prevented means nothing without knowing: What's the cost of untreated diabetes? What else could we do with that money? What threshold are we using? Let's talk about thresholds next."

## Poll 4: Decision Thresholds
**Timing:** After the threshold slide (Slide 11)

### Poll Question:
**If a program costs $75,000 per QALY gained (in the 'potentially cost-effective' zone), would your organization likely fund it?**

- Yes, probably
- Maybe, depends on other factors
- Probably not
- We don't use these thresholds in our decisions

### Discussion Prompt:
"For those who said 'depends on other factors,' what factors? This is where real decision-making happens. Thresholds are guidelines, not rules. Equity, community priorities, available alternatives all matter. Let's talk about when cost-effectiveness ISN'T the whole story."

## Poll 5: Beyond Cost-Effectiveness
**Timing:** After "Beyond CEA" slide (Slide 12)

### Poll Question:
**Have you ever supported (or seen support for) a program that wasn't the most cost-effective option?**

- Yes, for equity reasons
- Yes, for political or community reasons
- Yes, because there were no alternatives
- No, cost-effectiveness usually drives our decisions
- Unsure

### Discussion Prompt:
"Many of you have! This is normal and appropriate. Cost-effectiveness is one input, not the only input. If you're serving underserved populations, building strategic capacity, or fulfilling legal obligations, a higher cost per outcome may be justified. The key is being transparent about WHY."

## Poll 6: Measuring Outcomes
**Timing:** After the measurement problem slide (Slide 14)

### Poll Question:
**When you report program outcomes, what measure do you most commonly use?**

- Number of people served
- Cases prevented or detected
- Lives saved
- Hospitalizations or ED visits avoided
- QALYs or DALYs
- We don't measure health outcomes directly

### Discussion Prompt:
"County-level work often uses practical measures like cases prevented or visits avoided. That's appropriate and often more meaningful to your audience. QALYs are useful for cross-condition comparisons and published research, but you don't need them for every analysis. Match your measure to your audience and data."

---

## Poll 7: QALY Controversy
**Timing:** After QALY controversy slide (Slide 17)

### Poll Question:
**How comfortable are you using QALYs in your work?**

- Very comfortable, I understand the trade-offs
- Somewhat comfortable, but aware of limitations
- Uncomfortable, concerned about equity implications
- I'd prefer to use other measures when possible
- I need to learn more before deciding

### Discussion Prompt:
"This is genuinely contested in the field. Some economists strongly defend QALYs, others argue they embed bias against disability and aging. For county work, you can often avoid this debate by using natural units. When you do see QALY-based analyses, knowing this controversy helps you interpret them critically."

## Facilitator Notes

### Timing Guidelines
- Each poll: 30-45 seconds for voting
- Discussion: 2-3 minutes per poll
- Total polling time: ~25-30 minutes across the hour
- Leave buffer for technical issues

### Technical Tips
- Test Forms/Polls app before the webinar starts
- Have backup chat prompts if polls fail
- Assign a co-host to monitor chat and surface themes
- Save poll results for post-webinar analysis

### Engagement Best Practices
- Acknowledge specific responses ("I see many of you said...")
- Connect responses to upcoming content
- Validate diverse perspectives
- Avoid making anyone feel their answer was "wrong"
