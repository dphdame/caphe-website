## Poll 4: The Multiplier
**Timing:** After showing ROI examples (Slide 14)

### Poll Question:
**Which ROI example surprised you most?**

- Tobacco control ($48 return per $1)
- Lead poisoning prevention ($17-221 per $1)
- Childhood immunization ($7-24 per $1)
- Diabetes prevention ($5 per $1)
- None surprised me

### Discussion Prompt:
"The lead poisoning numbers often surprise people. Those returns include lifetime earnings, reduced special education costs, and avoided criminal justice involvement. This shows how much we undercount when we only look at healthcare savings. Societal returns are often much larger."

## Poll 6: Data Challenges
**Timing:** After "Common Challenges" slide (Slide 19)

### Poll Question:
**What's the biggest barrier to calculating ROI for your programs?**

- Finding data on costs avoided or savings
- Attributing outcomes to our specific program
- Choosing the right time horizon
- Getting buy-in to do the analysis at all
- Lack of staff time or expertise

### Discussion Prompt:
"Time and expertise come up a lot. The good news is you don't need to do original research. Published ROI studies can often be adapted to your local context. If tobacco control returns $48:1 nationally, you can make a reasonable case it's similar in your county."

## Facilitator Notes

### Timing Guidelines
- Each poll: 30-45 seconds for voting
- Discussion: 2-3 minutes per poll
- Total polling time: ~25-30 minutes across the hour
- Leave buffer for technical issues

### Special Notes for This Session
- This is the **final session** of the 2026 series. Acknowledge completion.
- **Budget officers may attend** this one more than previous sessions. Gauge the room.
- The **"Investment Not Expense"** framing is the key message. Return to it.
- Celebrate the **California examples**. Local pride matters.

### Handling Skepticism
Some attendees may push back on ROI:
- "You can't put a price on health" → Acknowledge, but note that budget decisions are made whether we provide numbers or not
- "These studies are cherry-picked" → Fair, but even conservative estimates show positive returns
- "My program is different" → Encourage adaptation, not wholesale rejection
