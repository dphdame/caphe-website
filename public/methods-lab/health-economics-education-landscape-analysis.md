# Health Economics Education Landscape Analysis
## Strategic Intelligence for CAPHE Methods Lab Launch

**Prepared:** December 31, 2025
**Scope:** Health economics and public health policy education for practitioners
**Target Audience:** County health department staff, policy analysts, public health economists

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Key Findings:**

1. **Significant Education Gap Exists**: 75% of health professionals consider their health economics knowledge insufficient, with a documented "curriculum gap" in applied, practitioner-focused training vs. academic programs.

2. **First-Mover Advantage in Practitioner Niche**: While academic programs (Harvard, Johns Hopkins, ISPOR) dominate the market, there is minimal competition for free, case-based, interactive modules specifically designed for California county health departments and policy practitioners.

3. **Optimal Launch Volume**: Successful educational hubs launch with 3-9 modules initially. Dell Medical School launched with 3 modules and achieved 130,098 pageviews from 8,546 users in 15 months, with 90% satisfaction. Arizona State launched with 100+ courses across specializations.

4. **Case-Based Learning is Critical Differentiator**: Research shows case-based learning (CBL) significantly outperforms traditional approaches for health economics education, with higher student satisfaction and engagement, particularly when integrated with real-world scenarios.

5. **Economic Pressure Creates Demand**: Local and state health departments cut 60,000 jobs from 2008-2012. Economic evaluation remains a competency gap in public health decision-making, making cost-effectiveness analysis skills increasingly essential for resource-constrained departments.

**Strategic Recommendation:**
Launch CAPHE Methods Lab with your current 2 modules, emphasizing the California-specific case study approach as a key differentiator. Plan rapid expansion to 6-8 modules within 6 months to establish thought leadership in this underserved niche.

## 1. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE ANALYSIS

### Major Players & Positioning

#### Academic Institutions (Established Players)

**Harvard University**
- **Offerings**: Health Care Economics course (6 weeks, ~20 hours), CAUSALab Summer Courses on Causal Inference, Causal Diagrams course
- **Target Audience**: Medical professionals, researchers, graduate students
- **Pricing**: Premium ($2,000-4,000 range for courses, some free audit options)
- **Differentiation**: Prestigious faculty (Miguel Hernán), AMA continuing education credits, theoretical depth
- **Weakness**: Expensive, academic focus vs. practitioner application
- **Sources**: [Harvard Online](https://harvardonline.harvard.edu/course/health-care-economics), [CAUSALab](https://hsph.harvard.edu/research/causalab/courses/)

**Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health**
- **Offerings**: MHS in Health Economics and Outcomes Research, Public Health Economics Certificate, PhD concentration
- **Target Audience**: Career-changers, industry professionals, doctoral candidates
- **Pricing**: Full graduate program tuition
- **Differentiation**: HEOR industry focus, prepares for pharma/payer roles
- **Weakness**: Degree-focused, not modular, expensive, industry vs. public health orientation
- **Source**: [Johns Hopkins HEOR](https://publichealth.jhu.edu/academics/mhs-in-health-economics-and-outcomes-research)

**University of York Centre for Health Economics**
- **Offerings**: Decision Modelling for Health Economic Evaluation (4 modules, 6 weeks), AMEEHTA Workshop (8 weeks)
- **Target Audience**: HTA analysts, managers, policymakers
- **Pricing**: Premium (£1,000-2,000+ range)
- **Differentiation**: Advanced methods, UK health technology assessment focus
- **Weakness**: UK-centric, advanced (not introductory), expensive

**Oxford HERC**

## 3. FIRST-MOVER ADVANTAGE ASSESSMENT

### Evidence of Market Gap

**Documented Education Gap**
- 75% of medical students/professionals consider their health economics knowledge insufficient
- 93% favor digital teaching formats (e-learning)
- Structural "curriculum gap" where economic aspects permeate all care levels but training is inadequate or delayed
- Student knowledge varies widely across institutions, with intensive curricula performing only marginally better
- **Sources**: [Digital Health Economics Education](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2025.1624053/full), [Health Economics Education Assessment](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20423258/)

**Practitioner-Specific Gap**
- Economic evaluation remains a competency gap in public health decision-making
- Local and state health departments cut 60,000 jobs (2008-2012), creating pressure for evidence-based resource allocation
- "To address this competency gap and prepare contemporary public health professionals, training for a public health profession is needed"
- Many practitioners work in professional isolation with limited access to up-to-date information
- **Sources**: [Economic Evaluation in Public Health](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4478374/), [Bridging the Education Gap](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK532784/)

**California-Specific Need**
- California has 58 counties, each with health departments requiring economic analysis capacity
- UCLA HEER Program specializes in large-scale California interventions (Whole Person Care, PRIME) but no statewide training infrastructure
- LA County explicitly uses cost-effectiveness analysis but training is limited
- Return on investment from $1 invested in California county public health: $67-$88
- **Sources**: [UCLA HEER](https://healthpolicy.ucla.edu/our-work/health-economics-and-evaluation-research-heer-program), [LA County CHIE](http://publichealth.lacounty.gov/chie/PA_PE_intro.htm)

### Competitive Timeline Analysis

**Established Players (10+ years)**
- ISPOR founded 1995, HEOR Learning Lab established ~2015
- Oxford HERC since 1996
- Harvard, Johns Hopkins long-standing academic programs
- **Implication**: These players are entrenched but serve different markets (industry, academia)

**Recent Entrants (2017-2025)**
- Dell Medical School VBHC modules (2017) - proof of concept for interactive, free modules
- Digital health economics education discussions emerging (2025 research)
- State learning hubs (Kentucky, Arizona) emerging as model (2024-2025)
- **Implication**: Trend toward free, applied, case-based learning is recent and accelerating

**Vacant Niche: California County Practitioner Training**
- No identified competitor specifically serving California county health departments with free, interactive, case-based health economics modules
- Closest: CDC (not California-specific), CAPHTC (not health economics-focused), UCLA HEER (research/evaluation, not training)
- **Window of Opportunity**: 2-3 years before either (a) competitors enter space or (b) gap closes naturally
- **First-Mover Advantages**:
  1. **Brand Association**: CAPHE becomes synonymous with "California health economics training"
  2. **Network Effects**: Early adopters from California's 58 counties become ambassadors
  3. **Content Moat**: Case studies based on California examples create differentiation that's hard to replicate
  4. **Partnership Opportunities**: Establish relationships with CAPHTC, county health departments, UC system before competitors
  5. **Data Advantage**: User learning data informs continuous improvement and new module topics

### Risk Assessment: Competitive Entry

**Low Risk (2-3 years)**:
- Academic institutions unlikely to pivot to free, California-specific practitioner training (conflicts with tuition model)
- ISPOR unlikely to offer free content (conflicts with membership/course revenue model)

**Medium Risk (3-5 years)**:
- Well-funded public health foundations could sponsor similar initiative
- UC system could create competing resource
- **Mitigation**: First-mover advantage, established user base, continuous improvement

**High Risk Trigger Events**:
- California Department of Public Health launches statewide training initiative
- Major foundation (RWJF, CHCF) funds competitive program
- **Mitigation**: Position CAPHE as preferred partner for any state/foundation initiative

## 4. DIFFERENTIATION FACTORS FOR SUCCESS

### Evidence-Based Success Factors

#### 1. Case-Based Learning (CBL) - Critical Differentiator

**Research Evidence:**
- Students' satisfaction with health economics topics was higher when incorporated as part of problem-based learning (PBL) pedagogy vs. traditional approaches
- Case-based learning uses guided inquiry with defined learning outcomes, more structured than PBL
- Students remained more attentive throughout case-based sessions vs. traditional lectures
- CBL impact ranges from knowledge gains to changing patient care outcomes
- Most effective when cases are based on patient/real-world scenarios integrating basic, social, and clinical sciences
- **Sources**: [CBL in Health Economics](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2382120519891178), [BEME Guide on CBL](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.3109/0142159X.2012.680939), [CBL Worldwide Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5736264/)

**CAPHE Advantage:**
- California case studies create immediate relevance for county practitioners
- Real county scenarios (vs. generic textbook examples) increase engagement
- Learners can directly apply methods to their own county contexts

**Implementation Recommendations:**
- Each module should center on 1-2 detailed California county case studies
- Include discussion prompts: "How would this apply in your county?"
- Provide downloadable case study templates practitioners can adapt

#### 2. Interactive Learning - High Engagement

**Research Evidence:**
- 93% of learners favor digital teaching formats (e-learning)
- Interactive lessons increase retention vs. passive reading
- Dell Medical School's interactive modules achieved 90% satisfaction
- Mixed modalities (live + recorded + interactive elements) outperform single-format delivery
- **Sources**: [Digital Health Economics Education](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2025.1624053/full), [Dell Medical School](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6727932/)

**CAPHE Advantage:**
- Interactive visualizations (already implemented in your modules)
- Embedded calculation tools
- Interactive decision trees

**Implementation Recommendations:**
- Add knowledge check quizzes after each section
- Include interactive cost-effectiveness calculators
- Create scenario-based simulations where users make decisions and see outcomes

#### 3. Applied vs. Academic Focus - Practitioner Needs

**Market Evidence:**
- Practitioners need ability to critically analyze others' empirical work AND perform basic analysis themselves
- Skills in demand: policy analysis (32% of job postings), public policies, economics, project management, policy development, data analysis
- Applied programs explicitly designed for "working professionals" outperform academic programs for practitioner audience
- Economic evaluation competency gap exists because academic programs focus on research vs. application
- **Sources**: [Applied Health Economics](https://www.jefferson.edu/academics/colleges-schools-institutes/population-health/degrees-programs/applied-health-economics.html), [Weill Cornell MS](https://phs.weill.cornell.edu/graduate-education-clinical-training/masters-track/ms-health-policy-and-economics), [Health Policy Analyst Skills](https://www.franklin.edu/career-guide/political-scientists/how-to-become-health-policy-analysts)

**CAPHE Advantage:**
- Focus on "how to do this in your county" vs. "theory of health economics"
- Teach sufficient technical skills for day-to-day analysis, not PhD-level econometrics
- Emphasize interpretation and communication of results to policymakers

**Implementation Recommendations:**
- Each module should end with "Take Action" section: specific steps practitioners can implement in their county
- Include templates (budget impact analysis template, cost-effectiveness framework, stakeholder communication brief)
- Provide "Technical Appendix" for those wanting deeper methods, but keep main content applied

#### 4. Free Access - Eliminating Barriers

**Market Evidence:**
- Freemium model dominates successful MOOC platforms (Coursera, edX)
- Paid certificates available but not required (Dell: 24% registration rate suggests users value free access)
- State-funded hubs (Kentucky, California Public Health Training Center) are free and widely used
- Financial aid requests on Coursera indicate cost is barrier for public health professionals
- **Sources**: [Free vs Paid Health Economics Courses](https://www.classcentral.com/subject/health-economics), [Kentucky Learning Hub](https://www.kentuckyteacher.org/news/2025/08/kentucky-department-of-education-launches-ky-learning-hub-for-high-quality-professional-learning/)

**CAPHE Advantage:**
- Zero cost barrier increases access across all California counties (rich and poor)
- Removes need for approval processes to purchase training
- Enables sharing within departments and peer recommendations

**Implementation Recommendations:**
- Maintain free model indefinitely for core modules
- Consider optional paid certification or continuing education credits (revenue stream if needed)
- Partner with professional associations (e.g., California Conference of Local Health Officers) for distribution

#### 5. California Context - Local Relevance

**Market Evidence:**
- Context-relevant case studies improve learning outcomes vs. generic examples
- iHEA Teaching SIG emphasizes "context-relevant courses"
- Alameda County Place Matters, LA County programs demonstrate demand for local policy analysis
- California has unique policy environment (Medi-Cal expansion, SB 50, PRIME/Whole Person Care)
- **Sources**: [iHEA Teaching](https://healtheconomics.org/sigs/the/), [Alameda County Place Matters](https://academic.oup.com/book/24509), [UCLA HEER California Programs](https://healthpolicy.ucla.edu/our-work/health-economics-and-evaluation-research-heer-program)

**CAPHE Advantage:**
- Case studies drawn from California's 58 counties create immediate recognition
- California-specific policy context (Medi-Cal, county health department structure, state funding mechanisms)
- Examples use California cost data, demographics, health outcomes

**Implementation Recommendations:**
- Explicitly title modules "California Methods Lab" or "California Health Economics"
- Include county-specific data comparisons (practitioners can see their county's metrics)
- Feature guest experts from California counties, UC system, state agencies

#### 6. Micro-Credentials and Stackable Learning - Motivation

**Market Evidence:**
- ASU Learning Hub offers micro-credentials with stackable courses
- Coursera certificates drive completion rates
- Professional development requirements in public health create demand for credentials
- Specializations (3-9 courses) provide goal-oriented learning paths
- **Sources**: [ASU Learning Hub](https://education.asu.edu/about/news/asu-launches-professional-learning-hub-educators), [Coursera Model](https://www.coursera.org/courses?query=health+economics)

**CAPHE Advantage:**
- Can offer "California Certified Health Economics Analyst" credential after completing module series
- Partner with UC schools or state agencies for official recognition
- Create specialization tracks ("Causal Inference Track," "Cost-Effectiveness Track," "Policy Analysis Track")

**Implementation Recommendations:**
- Design modules to stack into 3-4 specialization tracks
- Award digital badges for module completion (shareable on LinkedIn)
- Create capstone project requirement: practitioners apply methods to their own county and submit for review

## 5. AUDIENCE EXPECTATIONS & NEEDS

### Target Audience Characteristics

**County Health Department Staff**
- **Roles**: Epidemiologists, program managers, policy analysts, health officers
- **Pain Points**:
  - Need to justify program budgets with ROI analysis
  - Asked to conduct cost-effectiveness analyses without formal training
  - Must communicate economic impact to county supervisors (non-technical audience)
  - Limited time for multi-week courses or graduate programs
- **Learning Preferences**:
  - Self-paced (unpredictable work schedules)
  - Applied examples from peer counties
  - Templates and tools they can immediately use
  - Short modules (30-60 minutes) vs. multi-hour lectures
- **Success Metrics**: Can produce credible cost-benefit analysis for county board presentation
- **Sources**: [LA County CHIE](http://publichealth.lacounty.gov/chie/PA_PE_intro.htm), [Economic Evaluation Competency Gap](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4478374/)

**Public Health Policy Analysts**
- **Roles**: State/local government analysts, legislative staff, think tank researchers
- **Pain Points**:
  - Need to evaluate competing policy proposals quickly
  - Must understand economic evaluations produced by others (critical appraisal)
  - Required to produce policy briefs synthesizing economic evidence
  - Balancing economic efficiency with health equity considerations
- **Learning Preferences**:
  - Policy-relevant methods (budget impact analysis, distributional cost-effectiveness)
  - California policy examples (Medi-Cal, SB legislation, county programs)
  - Guidance on communicating uncertainty to policymakers
  - Access to peer network (other California analysts)
- **Success Metrics**: Can critique third-party economic evaluations and advise on policy implications
- **Sources**: [Health Policy Analyst Skills](https://www.franklin.edu/career-guide/political-scientists/how-to-become-health-policy-analysts), [Columbia Health Policy Analysis](https://www.publichealth.columbia.edu/academics/degrees/master-public-health/certificates/health-policy-analysis)

**Public Health Economists (Early-Career)**
- **Roles**: County/state health economists, program evaluators, research analysts
- **Pain Points**:
  - Learned theory in graduate school but need applied skills
  - Isolated in their county (no peer economists to consult)
  - Need continuing education to stay current on methods
  - Struggle with software implementation (Stata, R, Excel for CEA)
- **Learning Preferences**:
  - Methodologically rigorous but applied
  - Code examples and software tutorials
  - Connection to other California health economists
  - Advanced modules on specialized topics (value of information analysis, distributional CEA)
- **Success Metrics**: Can independently conduct economic evaluations meeting academic standards
- **Sources**: [Johns Hopkins HEOR](https://publichealth.jhu.edu/academics/mhs-in-health-economics-and-outcomes-research), [Applied Health Economics Programs](https://www.jefferson.edu/academics/colleges-schools-institutes/population-health/degrees-programs/applied-health-economics.html)

- **Competitive Landscape**: Harvard CAUSALab exists but academic focus; applied county practitioner version is white space

## 8. COMPETITIVE THREATS & MITIGATION

### Potential Competitive Threats

**Threat 1: UC System Launches Competing Platform**
- **Likelihood**: Medium (UC has resources and expertise)
- **Impact**: High (credibility, resources, distribution)
- **Mitigation**:
  - Partner with UC proactively (make CAPHE the UC-endorsed platform)
  - Position CAPHE as practitioner complement to UC's academic programs
  - Recruit UC faculty as guest contributors (creates buy-in)

**Threat 2: California Department of Public Health (CDPH) Develops In-House Training**
- **Likelihood**: Low-Medium (budget constraints, capacity limitations)
- **Impact**: High (state authority, mandated use)
- **Mitigation**:
  - Position CAPHE as CDPH's training partner (CAPHE develops, CDPH endorses/distributes)
  - Demonstrate cost savings vs. CDPH building from scratch
  - Offer to customize modules for CDPH priorities

**Threat 3: National Foundation Funds Competing Initiative**
- **Likelihood**: Low (niche market)
- **Impact**: Medium-High (resources, national reach)
- **Mitigation**:
  - First-mover advantage: build user base and brand before competitor emerges
  - California-specific content creates switching costs
  - Pursue partnership with foundation (CAPHE as implementation partner)

**Threat 4: ISPOR or Academic Institution Offers Free Content**
- **Likelihood**: Low (conflicts with revenue model)
- **Impact**: Medium (credibility, content quality)
- **Mitigation**:
  - Differentiate on practitioner focus vs. academic/industry focus
  - California-specific case studies create unique value
  - Faster iteration and responsiveness to user needs vs. slower academic processes

## 10. CONCLUSION: STRATEGIC POSITIONING

### Core Value Proposition

**CAPHE Methods Lab is the only free, interactive, case-based health economics training platform designed specifically for California county health departments and public health policy practitioners.**

### Competitive Moats (Defensible Advantages)

1. **California Specificity**: Case studies, data, policy context create switching costs
2. **Network Effects**: More users → more case study contributions → more valuable platform
3. **First-Mover**: Brand association with "California health economics training"
4. **Applied Focus**: Practitioner orientation vs. academic competitors
5. **Free Access**: Eliminates cost barrier competitors rely on for revenue

### Critical Success Factors

1. **Rapid Expansion to 6-8 Modules**: Achieve minimum viable volume within 6 months
2. **Distribution Partnerships**: CAPHTC, CCLHO, CDPH for reach to all 58 counties
3. **Case-Based Differentiation**: California examples in every module
4. **Interactive Tools**: Calculators, templates, frameworks practitioners can use immediately
5. **Community Building**: Forum, office hours, peer learning to reduce isolation
6. **Credentialing**: Certificate program for professional development value

### Market Window: Act Now

- **Education gap is documented and acute** (75% insufficient knowledge, competency gap in public health)
- **No direct competitor** serving California county practitioner niche
- **Trend toward free, applied, case-based learning** is recent (2017-2025) and accelerating
- **Budget pressures** on counties create demand for cost-effectiveness analysis skills
- **2-3 year window** before competitor entry likely

### Final Recommendation

**Launch immediately with 2 modules, emphasizing "Phase 1" positioning. Commit to monthly module releases to reach 6-8 modules by September 2025. Secure CAPHTC and CCLHO partnerships within 90 days. Prioritize California case studies and interactive tools as core differentiators. Build toward 1,500 users and 45-county reach within 12 months.**

CAPHE Methods Lab has identified and can own a valuable niche in the health economics education market. The combination of practitioner focus, California specificity, free access, and case-based interactivity creates a defensible competitive position. Success requires rapid execution to capitalize on the first-mover advantage before the market window closes.
