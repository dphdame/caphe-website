# Antigravity Prompt: Hierarchy of Evidence in Study Design

## Prompt

Clean, professional staircase/ladder infographic on white background. Four ascending rectangular steps arranged diagonally from bottom-left to top-right, showing progression from weaker to stronger study designs:

**Step 1 (Bottom, smallest):** "Cross-Sectional" in light gray (#BDBDBD) with dark text
- Subtitle below step: "Snapshot comparison"
- Small circle with "1" inside

**Step 2:** "Pre-Post" in medium gray (#9E9E9E) with white text
- Subtitle: "Before and after"
- Small circle with "2" inside

**Step 3:** "Difference-in-Differences" in teal (#0D9488) with white text
- Subtitle: "Compare the change"
- Small circle with "3" inside

**Step 4 (Top, largest):** "Randomized Trial" in dark blue (#0041A5) with white text
- Subtitle: "Random assignment"
- Small circle with "4" inside

**Left side vertical label:** "Weaker Evidence" at bottom, "Stronger Evidence" at top, with upward arrow

**Right side annotation box:** Small callout box in gold (#F9A825) border with text "But what matters is IDENTIFICATION" pointing between Steps 3 and 4

**Bottom annotation:** Italicized text: "Epidemiologists: 'Use strongest available' | Economists: 'What can we identify?'"

Style: Flat design, no gradients except subtle depth on steps. Minimalist infographic style. Montserrat font, semi-bold weight for step labels, regular for subtitles. Each step slightly overlaps the one below it to create depth. Clean modern aesthetic.

## Lab Context

This image supports Lab 12: Hierarchy of Evidence in Study Design (Validity & Solutions tier).

The key message: Epidemiologists frame study design as "use the strongest available." Economists reframe as "what variation in treatment identifies the causal effect?" The ladder is a useful heuristic, but identification is the real goal.
