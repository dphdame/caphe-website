How This Works

This calculator uses the coefficient from Brown (2016), which found that a $10 per capita increase in public health spending reduces mortality by 9.16 deaths per 100,000 population. The economic value is calculated using the EPA's Value of Statistical Life ($10 million).

Source: Brown, T.T. (2016). "How effective are public health departments at preventing mortality?" Economics & Human Biology, 20, 159-172.

Behind the Numbers

Methodology

📊

Causal Inference

Uses Lewbel instrumental variables to address endogeneity and identify the causal effect of public health spending on mortality.

📈

Panel Data

Based on California county-level panel data from 2003-2023, covering all 57 counties with controls for demographics and economic conditions.

✅

Peer-Reviewed

The underlying methodology was published in Economics & Human Biology and has been replicated with 99.4% accuracy.

Technical Details

The calculator implements a simplified version of the analysis from Brown (2016), which uses Lewbel (2012) instrumental variables to address potential endogeneity in public health spending. The key identification assumption is that the variance of the error term differs across counties in ways unrelated to mortality outcomes.

The main coefficient of -9.16 deaths per 100,000 per $10 per capita investment has been replicated using California county data from 2003-2023. Economic value calculations use the EPA's Value of Statistical Life ($10 million, 2020 dollars).

Important Context

Limitations & Caveats

⚠️

Average Effect

The coefficient represents an average effect across all public health activities. Specific interventions may have higher or lower returns.

⚠️

Historical Data

Estimates are based on historical relationships. Future returns may differ due to changes in health conditions, technology, or policy environment.

⚠️

Mortality Only

The model focuses on mortality reduction. Public health activities also improve quality of life, which is not captured in these estimates.

⚠️

Not for Formal Evaluation

These estimates are for preliminary planning and advocacy. Formal cost-effectiveness studies require program-specific data and analysis.
