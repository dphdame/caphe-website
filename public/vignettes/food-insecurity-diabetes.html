4 Sensitivity Analysis: The E-Value

How strong would an unmeasured confounder need to be to explain away our finding? The E-value quantifies the robustness of our estimate to hidden bias.

Confounding Strength Required

Hypothetical Unmeasured Confounder

Suppose we missed a confounder like "Health Literacy." How strongly would it need to be associated with both food insecurity and diabetes hospitalizations to explain our result?

1.0
E-Value for Current Estimate
2.14
An unmeasured confounder would need to be associated with both food insecurity and diabetes hospitalizations by a risk ratio of 2.14 to explain away the observed effect.

What Does This Mean?

The E-value provides a threshold: if no plausible unmeasured confounder could have this strong a relationship with both variables, our finding is robust. Compare to known confounders: smoking and lung cancer have a risk ratio around 10; obesity and diabetes around 3.

Rationale

Given the ambiguity in the causal evidence, we recommend a rigorous pilot study before scaling interventions. This approach allows for:

  • Causal identification through randomization or quasi-experimental design
  • Cost-effectiveness estimation with credible counterfactuals
  • Learning about implementation challenges before full rollout

Proposed Design

Implement a staggered rollout of food prescription programs across willing counties, with careful documentation of timing to enable difference-in-differences analysis.

Data Requirements

  • Food prescription program enrollment and utilization data
  • Individual-level linkage to hospital discharge data where possible
