Methodology

This analysis estimates how public health spending affects mortality rates and translates that effect into lives saved and economic value.

The underlying research uses Lewbel IV estimation on California county panel data (2003-2023) to identify the causal effect of public health spending on mortality rates.

Key Finding: Each $10 per capita in public health spending reduces mortality by 9.16 deaths per 100,000 population.

For context: if a county of 1 million people increases spending by $10 per person, approximately 92 fewer deaths would occur annually.

Social value is calculated using the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services Value of Statistical Life (VSL): $13.6 million (2024 dollars).

Limitations

These estimates are based on historical county-level data. Actual results may vary based on program implementation. The coefficient represents an average effect across all public health activities; specific interventions may have higher or lower returns.

This report is designed for presentations to boards, budget committees, and other stakeholders. Full methodology is available in the attached working paper.
